Today's news

A very rusty re-write

As you may know, Rust is a memory-safe and extremely fast programming langauge which first appared in 2015. Since then, it had gained a lot of traction and it has become an in-joke amongst the programming community to re-write entire codebases in Rust, and just recently Fish has succumbed to this.
On January 28th, 2023, a pull request was submitted and merged into the Fish shell's repository. Now that the new year 2025 has rolled around, the devs over at the Fish GitHub repo have released beta version 4.0, a complete re-write of the entire Fish codebase in Rust.
Fish is absolutely no stranger to codebase re-writes, as in 2009, it was re-written from C to C++. However, the developers seem to have the grievances about the programming language. To quote their blog post, "Header files are annoying [and] templates are complicated" Hence, a re-write has been expected for some time now.
This re-write has been no small task for Fish's contributors, as there have been 1155 files changed, 110247 insertions(+), 88941 deletions(-) (excluding translations). This is no small task and has taken about 2 years of work to do so. This shows that the "rewrite it in Rust" has gone from a simple in-joke to a large project done by hundreds of dedicated authors. Clearly, this is not just a few changes but rather a huge leap forward for the Fish shell and its many users. I applaud the contributors for proceeding with such a massive undertaking.

A sticky-sweet lawsuit

Honey is a free browser extension that claims to automatically apply coupon codes to a product once someone buys it, making it cheaper. However, Honey has come under fire, initally by MegaLag through this video calling out Honey's alleged shady and deceptive behaviour. Now that this has been brought to the attention of a a lawyer and popular YouTube content creator, Devin J. Stone, who goes by LegalEagle, has recently filed a class-action lawsuit against the browser extension.
A content creator would have promoted Honey and directed its audience to a referral link¹, and once an interest consumer uses it, Honey may or may not give you working coupon codes and at the check-out at the last second before you pay for whatever product you had wished to buy through some eCommerce service in a way, Honey removes attribution to the creator and takes whatever money the content creator would have received for themselves.
The coupon codes are also an issue, as manually searching for coupon codes usually gives better results at better values than Honey's. In their podcast, they said they want to control the customer's journey through the check-out by giving subpar first-party coupons as it allows them to make more money than if they were using already existing coupon codes. According to the lawsuit, this would be a clear-cut example of fraud as instead of placing power into the hands of the consumers as they have promise, they have instead placed more power into the hands of the businesses and especially themselves.
This is no small lawsuit, as 30 billion dollars have been circulating through Google's AdSense. It is unclear as to how much money has gone to Honey but it is reasonable to assume that a few billion have gone directly to fill the pockets of PayPal and the maintainers of the Honey browser extension. This lawsuit alone would leave a significant mark in the world's legal history. If Honey wins, businesses would be able to freely commit "coupon fraud". If Honey loses, however, this would show that ultimately, it is the consumers who can give or take power from businesses.

Notes:

¹ A referral link is a link that creators use when promoting a product to direct you to it. You, the user of said referral link will usually receive some "reward" for clicking on the referral link, which is usually some sort of discount or free trial, and the content creator who promoted it will usually receive something of value, whether it be free products or money.
